### **Slide 1: Title & Introduction (1 min)**
* **Topic:** The American Phenomenon of Pedestrian Right-of-Way and Traffic Culture.
* **The Hook:** Introduce the initial culture shock many international or visiting students face when cars unexpectedly stop for them at intersections, even without traffic lights.
* **Thesis:** Everyday traffic interactions reflect deeper cultural values regarding safety and power dynamics, though recent political changes and local environments create friction between the "rules" and reality.

### **Slide 2: Our Methodology & Interviewees (1 min)**
* **Who we interviewed:** Two local drivers to get a ground-level perspective on how traffic conventions are learned and practiced. 
    * *Interviewee 1:* A driver with 7 years of experience who took their driving test in downtown San Francisco. 
    * *Interviewee 2:* A local driver familiar with the specific driving environment of the Pacific Northwest and the Bay Area.
* **Our Questions:** Focused on the motivations behind yielding, the concept of "defensive driving," and reactions to the 2023 jaywalking legalization. 

### **Slide 3: Cultural Forces: The Pedestrian Pedestal (2 mins)**
* **The Unwritten Rule:** Drivers are taught to prioritize pedestrian safety above all else, slowing down even when pedestrians are 30 to 50 feet away. 
* **Defensive Driving:** Explain this cultural concept where drivers are taught to let others make the first move. 
* **The Power Dynamic:** Introduce a quote or soundbite from your first interview here. 
    > *"The driver has the greater power... you have to be more responsible with your action, no matter who this person is walking."*

### **Slide 4: Political Forces: The 2023 Jaywalking Law (2 mins)**
* **The Policy:** In 2023, California passed a law legalizing jaywalking when there is no immediate danger. 
* **Driver Anxiety:** Discuss how this top-down political decision impacts the everyday experience of the driver. Both interviewees noted this makes driving riskier and requires hyper-awareness.
* **The Urban vs. Rural Divide:** Mention Interviewee 1's nuanced take that this rule makes sense on empty roads but should probably still be banned or heavily enforced in dense, high-traffic cities like San Francisco.

### **Slide 5: The Local Reality: Theory vs. Practice (2 mins)**
* **The Ideal vs. The Real:** While the rules dictate ultimate respect for pedestrians, the local environment shifts this behavior. 
* **The Late-Night Rush:** Highlight Interviewee 2's observation that drivers often become impatient and rush through intersections late at night.
* **Student Behavior:** Use this slide to talk about the specific pedestrian culture around the university.
    > *"Here in Berkeley, yes, I've seen how like students... are bold to just go even if they see a car coming."*

### **Slide 6: Conclusion (1 min)**
* **Summarize:** How a simple act—waiting at a crosswalk—is a complex intersection of defensive driving education, legal liability (fines and license confiscation), and local community pacing.
* **Final Thought:** The 2023 law shows that American traffic culture is still evolving, constantly trying to balance the efficiency of the driver with the safety and freedom of the pedestrian.
