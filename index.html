The American Phenomenon:
Pedestrian Right-of-Way & Traffic Culture

Navigating the Unspoken Rules of the Road

Bohan Ding, Jiacun Sun, Jiaxuan Zhang, Xiangyu Wang

Group Presentation
April 2026

Methodology & Interviewees

  • Our Goal: To understand if yielding to pedestrians is a strict legal requirement or a cultural habit.
  • Interviewee 1 (Curtis): A driver with 7 years of experience who took his test in downtown San Francisco.
  • Interviewee 2: A local driver familiar with driving dynamics in Berkeley and the Pacific Northwest.

Cultural Forces: Defensive Driving

  • The Concept: "Defensive driving" means letting others on the road react and make the first move.
  • Mutual Acknowledgment: Pedestrians and drivers often wave hands at each other to show appreciation.
  • Power Dynamics: The driver operates a heavy vehicle with greater power, bearing ultimate responsibility.
"When you have the greater power, you have to be more responsible with your action..."

Political Forces: The 2023 Jaywalking Law

  • The Policy: California legalized jaywalking in 2023 when there is no immediate danger.
  • Driver Anxiety: This policy increases risk, requiring drivers to be constantly alert.
  • Urban vs. Rural: Drivers feel this rule is acceptable for empty highways but should be restricted in dense cities.

Theory vs. Local Reality: The Berkeley Experience

  • Late-Night Rush: Drivers can become impatient and rush through intersections late at night.
  • Bold Pedestrians: Young people and students in Berkeley often cross boldly, even with oncoming traffic.
  • Regional Shifts: Drivers in the Northwest wait patiently, whereas downtown drivers act more rushed.

Conclusion

  • A Complex Intersection: Yielding is a blend of traffic regulations and spontaneous community behavior.
  • Safety First: The culture recognizes that safety takes precedence, reinforced by severe penalties.
  • Ongoing Evolution: Traffic culture constantly balances driver efficiency with pedestrian freedom.
